week 09:

Random Walks in Biology

Hitting times for unbiased random walks

In the w09 lecture, we calculated the hitting time or time to capture, that is the expected time that it would take to reach a particular position \(i\), starting at \(t=0\).

Our result indicated that for an unbias random walk (\(p=q=1/2\)), it would take on average infinite time to reach any position. That result relied on the recursive equation for the hitting time \(h(i\mid 0)\),

\[h(i\mid 0) = 1 + \frac{1}{2}\ h(i+1\mid 0) + \frac{1}{2}\ h(i-1\mid 0),\]

which is based on the Markov recursion for an unbiased random walk,

and on the conditions that there were no boundaries for the particle to walk to, that is,

\[-\infty\leq i \leq + \infty\]

Under different boundary conditions, we obtain finite hitting times even for an unbiased random walk.

For a 1D unbiased random walk, in which particles can move between two positions \(a\leq i \leq b\), calculate the average hitting time that it would take starting at \(i\), to reach either \(a\) or \(b\).
